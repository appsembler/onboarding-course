As you are getting familiar with OpenCraft's culture and way of working, it also a good time to pass on a bit of wisdom, from other team members who have been in your situation before. The on-boarding can take a lot of effort, and can be somewhat overwhelming at times. So if you ever find yourself doubting your ability to make it, don't forget that it takes time for everyone to learn the ropes during the first months, and it's expected that it will consume part of your time on tasks. Don't be afraid to account for this!

  • We all felt like impostors at first
  • The hour tracking won't be used against you. It's used to bill clients and ensure we stay within project budgets, not to monitor individual performance. Log all the hours, even if they don't feel legitimate - or you are going to resent it in the long term. Also, clients don't see the individual log messages - they only see the title of the tickets on which the time is logged.
  • Individual performance is actually measured on elements like communication, anticipation of blockers, finding solutions within the given timeframes, asking for help, etc. At first, it can look useless or even spammy to just drop small replies onto issues, out of fear of wasting other people's time. But even the little, "Yes, thank you, I hear you" messages are important, and help keep the lines open.

Some quotes from other team members:

Brandon:

I partially thought [worklogs] were in place to specifically be used against me. It took me some time to fully come to terms with the hourly stuff. Most of the guilt/frustration went away the more I:

  • Trusted this stuff wasn't being used against me.
  • Saw that other people on the team struggling with tech stuff and spillover like I was - I think it's important to figure out what "normal" really is on a team. It's not usually the superhuman thing one thinks it is initially.

Jill:

I also struggled with communication on tasks. I'd find myself buried under days of pain before I came up for air and declared defeat, instead of reaching out early with questions. It's hard to ask for help, and even harder when you haven't met the people, and every word is indelibly printed on the page.

I docked my hours when I felt like my struggles were the fault of my own incompetence, and resented the job for "making" me do it. I viewed the OpenCraft team members as superhuman — the amount of work you guys get done, and how quickly and well you do it? Freaking amazing. The level of scrutiny performed on even the smallest ticket, and the quality of the code we produce, is incredible. I've never even had a job where my code was reviewed, let alone criticised line by line, and definitely not on public, open-source websites. Initially, when I started this job, I'd have to take a deep breath before replying with a "thank you for spotting that!", but now, I love seeing how clean the resulting code is once it's been through our wringer. I'm proud of what I create, and prouder now when it survives the reviews. But that took time.
